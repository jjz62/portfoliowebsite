    • ClubView - A Club Database

    Overview

    Worked alongside a team of developers, designers, and product managers to create a website that centralizes key information for Cornell student organizations to simplify the search and application process for students.

    Role

    Designer

    Timeline

    October 2021 - May 2022

    Skills/Tools

    Ideation, Prototyping, Motion Design, Figma

    Problem Space

    Positively Impacting our Community

    It is difficult to navigate the hundreds of clubs at Cornell.

    There are lots of events and deadlines to tediously keep up with. Students will often miss information.

    Students often miss deadlines or find out too late for recruitment.

    Particularly in Cornell's pre-professional culture, club recruitment and maintaining applications to student organizations is often stressful and demanding.

    Some organizations need more visibility.

    Many student organizations are unheard of due to the vast depth of different clubs on campus.

    User Groups

    Who can we help?

    After acknowledging the common problem many students had at Cornell, we narrowed our target users into two distinctly defined user groups.

    Club Owners: Students on the leadership team of a club who would use Clubview as a representative of their organization.

    Club Seekers: Students looking to browse through student organizations.

    The Product

    We decided to make something.

    To address the problems found, Design & Tech Initiative, a Cornell project team, started the Clubview team aiming to improve the user experience of searching for and applying to clubs at Cornell by centralizing key club information into a common web platform.

    🎓 Students

    Students can have an easier time club-searching using current information for a smoother recruitment process.

    🖥 Clubs

    Clubs can have a platform that is easy to update and can increase visibility.

    Our team drafted an information hierarchy to combine the features we outlined as most important. We prioritized visibility of the home page which would show a large variety of different clubs to search from by making it our landing page. We also housed a majority of the features under the menu to make them intuitive and all listed in one place for ease of access.

    Ideation

    Visualizing a Club

    From low-fi sketches, I began to conecptualize how individual clubs would be shown on Clubview.

    The low-fi layout chosen to iterate upon for its straightforward structuring of content which highlights elements of most importance such as name, links, and the favoriting clubs feature.

    I decided to seperate between a left and right side to distribute different key components to the page. The right side allows users to scroll through larger pieces of content such as descriptions and images. The static left side displays more vital information such as name, links, and other categories so that they won't move and will always be visible.

    There were many rounds of iterations to adjust both the visual branding and layout of the individual club page. Below are a few of the mockups that were developed during the iteration process.

    Mid-fi iterations on the individual club page that were adjusted to reduce visual clutter and have a more modern interface.

    The individual clubpage went thorugh mid-fi and high-fi iterating to arrive at this final design. Important functions such as accessing links to various club resources or favoriting the club by clicking the heart were highlighted, and the design itself was centered around a pink palette as a bright color to draw attention and demonstrate the excitement of student organizations.

    While previous iterations applied a large variety of colors used frequently, in developing the current high-fi designs I focused on applying color to emphasize specific calls-to-action to create a less visually crowded page where important features are more visible.

    Ideation

    Editing a Club

    Editing was first conceptualized so that users could edit directly on the page.

    Users would change to Editing Mode and select specific cards to edit so that they could visualize the page as a whole.

    So, why change?

    While the benefits of in-page editing included better visualization and a similar UI to match viewing a page, we ultimately came to the conclusion to change how club owners edit.

    ❌ Less Intuitive: Understanding the format of editing is not as intuitive in comparison to typical practice for editing through a form, which could create confusion.

    ❌ More Clicks: Moving to edit small individual pieces of content such as links would require lots more clicks to move through and seperate screens, which would discourage users.

    ❌ Prioritizng a Clear User Flow: Accessing and exiting editing mode might not be clear due to the similarity between the page as a viewer and editor. Furthermore, actions to take aren't clear.

    Editing as a seperate form.

    After rounds of iteration, I decided upon a seperate form with different sections for different pieces of content on the page broken down into 4 categories: Profile, Recruitment Banner, Description, and Events. To make a clear differentiation to viewing I used a complementary blue shade to distinguish editing actions.

    Users enter the editing form through the menu and can move through the different sections to change information.

    Conclusion

    Being immersed in a full team

    This project was my first and longest experience in a full product team and extremely valuable for the same reason. Some of the lessons that have stuck with me the most from the experience include:

    1. Documentation is super important.

    Working with developers to implement features I created, as well as working with other designers, really emphasized the importance of good documentation for me. It made handoff and teamwork in general so much easier and allowed processes to flow much more smoothly.

    2. When I'm feeling stuck, outside input is the best medicine.

    Whenever I'd feel stuck in my designs, the best rememdy was always outside input. When working on a design for so long its so easy to loose sight of easy and simple solutions as details get more convoluted, which is why outside help can sometimes be such a breath of fresh air. This experience taught me the importance of reaching out for help when I need it.

    3. Trust yourself.

    As a relatively new designer when first joining the team, it took me a long time to find my voice and advocate for my designs. The other designers I learned from emphasized the importance of trusting myself and being confident!
